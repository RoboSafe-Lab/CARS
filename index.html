Abstract

Adversarial scenario generation is critical for validating autonomous driving (AD) systems, yet current methods face intertwined challenges: selecting the appropriate adversary in complex multi-agent traffic, generating diverse attack behaviours, and determining whether a resulting collision is genuinely the AD system's fault. We present CARS (Context-Aware, Responsibility-attributed Scenario generation), an adversarial scenario generation framework with integrated responsibility attribution.

In CARS, a Gaussian Mixture Diffusion Model, pre-trained on naturalistic driving data and refined through reinforcement learning, generates behaviourally diverse adversarial trajectories against a target AD system. A learned selection module dynamically identifies the most threatening surrounding vehicle at every simulation step, ensuring a coherent and sustained adversarial interaction. A careful and competent driver model (CCDM) enables responsibility attribution: collisions that even a careful and competent driver could not have avoided are not attributed to the AD system under test.

On the nuScenes urban driving dataset, CARS generates 408 collision scenarios spanning diverse adversarial encounters while maintaining realistic driving dynamics. A CCDM attributes 398 (97.5%) to the target ADS. The learned adversarial policy transfers zero-shot across road topologies and geographical regions, from North American urban roads to European roundabouts and East Asian highways, with only a lightweight agent-selection classifier refitted per dataset. By unifying adversarial generation with regulatory-grade responsibility attribution, CARS provides a principled methodology for certifying the safety of autonomous driving systems, narrowing the gap between simulation-based testing and real-world regulatory acceptance.

Cross-dataset Transfer

The same frozen CARS policy, trained only on nuScenes urban traffic, is applied to two naturalistic drone datasets with contrasting scene geometries. The diffusion generator and target-model checkpoints are held fixed; only a lightweight agent-selection classifier is refitted on each dataset's labelled target–adversary pairs. Both videos are rendered on the original drone photo backgrounds provided with each dataset.

AD4CHE highway traffic
Frozen nuScenes policy applied to a Chinese highway drone recording. The context-aware module continues to identify the most threatening surrounding vehicle, and 94.9% of the generated collisions on AD4CHE pass the FSM responsibility check.
RounD roundabout
Frozen nuScenes policy applied to a four-arm roundabout drone recording at Neuweiler, Germany, a scene geometry absent from the training distribution. 81.3% of the generated collisions on RounD still pass the FSM responsibility check.

Context-aware Adversary Selection

A histogram gradient-boosting classifier re-evaluates all surrounding vehicles at every simulation step; a temporal confirmation gate (Kconf=5) suppresses transient ranking errors so the adversary role tracks the evolving scene. The same selection mechanism transfers to unseen scene geometries when retrained on each dataset's labelled target–adversary pairs.

Responsibility Attribution

Each generated collision is replayed with a reference driver model controlling the target; the scenario is retained only if the reference driver still fails to avoid impact. Attribution is cross-validated under three reference models: FSM (UN R157 Annex 5, primary CCDM), CC-JP (UN R157 Annex 4 Appendix 3, permissive human-driver baseline), and RSS (conservative formal safety envelope).

Validity columns report the percentage of each method's real-collision scenarios classified as attributable to the target under the three reference models. Hcrit is the normalised Shannon entropy of the FSM Hard/Medium/Easy distribution (higher is more balanced). infeas% is the scenario-averaged per-step violation rate of human-driver physical bounds (|a|>8 m/s², |jerk|>50 m/s³, |alat|>8 m/s²). CARS is the only method that simultaneously achieves high attribution (97.5% FSM), balanced severity coverage (Hcrit=0.909), and fully compliant adversary trajectories (infeas=0.00%). Each baseline fails on a different axis: Bezier-CAT and STRIVE drive the adversary beyond physical limits; SafeSim concentrates severity onto the Hard tier; STRIVE also loses two-thirds of its collisions under FSM as unattributable.
